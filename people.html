Summer School Mentors

Chris Fryer

 

CCS-2 Computational Physics and Methods

Laboratory Fellow & E. O. Lawrence Award Laureate

 

Chris Fryer is a computational astrophysicist working on a wide variety of astronomy problems from the formation of the moon to the progenitors and mechanisms behind the most powerful explosions in the universe:  gamma-ray bursts and supernovae.  Although he has spent time building new high-performance computer systems, his emphasis has been on developing the physics codes that use these systems to better understand these astrophysical phenomena.  In this effort, he has worked with a variety of codes and techniques to find the best methods to solve these multi-physics problems.

Wes Even

 

CCS-2 Computational Physics and Methods

 

Wes is a computational physicist focused on applying radiation hydrodynamics simulations to astrophysics and high energy density physics problems.  His research has focused primarily on compact binary mergers and supernovae.

At LANL Wes has been a member of a variety of code development and verification and validation projects.  He is currently a deputy project lead for the Next Generation Code Project and is leading the particle hydrodynamics team within that project.

Geoff Womeldorff

 

CCS-7 Applied Computer Science

 

Geoff is a compuational scientist with a background in mathematics and centroidal Voronoi tessellations. He has experience in numerical methods and parallel frameworks for multi-scale models for ocean, and algorithms for communication aggregation, thereof. His interests also include the coupling between proxy applications, their hosts, and programming models, codesign interactions, and parallel frameworks and algorithms, in general.

Josh Dolence

 

CCS-2 Computational Physics and Methods

 

Josh is a computational astrophysicist with expertise in the physics and numerical techniques required to study extreme astrophysical phenomena.  He has been particularly focused on understanding black hole accretion flows and core-collapse supernovae, and has developed several state-of-the-art, massively parallel, multi-physics codes he uses to simulate these systems.  Among other projects, Josh is now developing methods and algorithms for radiation transport coupled to mesh-free hydrodynamics, including smoothed particle hydrodynamics.

 

Robert Pavel

 

CCS-7 Applied Computer Science

Nicholas C. Metropolis Fellow

 

Robert is a computational scientist with a background in electrical and computer engineering. He has experience in improving the performance of multi-scale physics applications through the use of task based models and NoSQL databases. His interests include evaluating the suitability of modern task based programming models with respect to scientific applications.

 

Robert is an alumnus of the 2013 and 2014 summer school classes!

Oleg Korobkin

 

CCS-2 Computational Physics and Methods

Director's Fellow

 

Oleg is a computational physicist with a background in numerical relativity and relativistic astrophysics, with a particular focus on physics of neutron stars and accretion disks around black holes. He also has experience in simulating nucleosynthesis of heavy elements by solving complex nuclear reaction networks. He is interested in the inner workings of gamma-ray bursts, specifically in modeling violent relativistic hydrodynamics of neutron star mergers and investigating a variety of potentially observable signals.
